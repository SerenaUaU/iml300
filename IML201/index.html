Image Project

In my image project, I focused on how women are shaped by expectations around productivity, life milestones, and social roles.

Interpellation, which is “to be hailed or called in a way in which you recognize yourself to be the person intended by the call.” (Sturken & Cartwright, 2018, p.52)

Project 2

Digital Arugument

Boys Don't Cry: The Discipline of Masculinity

In my digital argument on toxic masculinity, I extended this idea further. I examined how masculinity is constructed through repetition in media and reinforced through systems of power. Masculinity is a performance shaped by expectations of dominance, control, and emotional restraint. These expectations not only harm men’s mental health, but also reinforce gender inequality and increase the risk of violence against women.

Through this project, I began to see identity as both socially constructed and deeply tied to power.

“Visuality, looking, and the gaze have been key factors in the shaping of power, the forms of power dynamics, and resistances to power structures through countervisuality.” (Sturken & Cartwright, 2018, pp.133).

Project 3

Surveillance Project

Me

In the surveillance project, I went back to explore my own identity. As an international student, I started with the question: why do I feel like I am not myself anymore when I switch language? on how women are shaped by expectations around productivity, life milestones, and social roles.

through the framework of surveillance, the gaze, and Foucault’s Panopticon, I realized that language itself functions as a form of surveillance. I constantly monitor how I speak, and how I might be perceived. Now, identity is something imposed from the inside. I’ve internalized the standards and started self-surveillance.

“Visuality, looking, and the gaze have been key factors in the shaping of power, the forms of power dynamics, and resistances to power structures through countervisuality.”

As Sturken and Cartwright (2018) describe in their analysis of Foucault, “docile bodies” are "citizens who uphold a society’s ideologies and laws by participating in an economy of discipline, internalizing conformity and improving themselves as a way to maintain the state." (112)

Who Am I?

I see identity as something continuously produced, through interpellation, through power structures, through representation, and through internalized surveillance, especially through language.

This course has changed not just how I understand identity, but also how I see the world. I now see behind the veil and explore how a phenomenon is formed. What systems of power is be working behind, and how am I participating in it? And this might be the most important shift, learning how I came to be.
